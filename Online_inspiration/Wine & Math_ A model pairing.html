A Model Pairing

by Lars Verspohl

Mention predictive modeling to the general public and you’re likely to conjure memes of complex mathematical equations swirling. Mention wine, and you get a much different reaction. One can be intimidating, the other inviting.

In this piece, we’re going to try to close that gap. We’ll build a statistical model trying to predict a wine’s quality by its properties. So grab some liquid courage in your favorite aged grape variety and get ready for MATH.

How to describe a wine

Before diving into building any predictive model we might want to define what we predict.

In our case, let's note that "wine quality" is a highly imprecise term. “Quality” to a sommelier might mean that a wine has legs and benefits from aeration. “Quality” to you might mean it’s under $10, with a twist off top.

A quality predicting model is unlikely to rank every wine out there on a universally approved scale. Our output — or quality ranking — needs to be neatly defined and depends on our inputs — or the wine parameters we’re using to establish a baseline.

The inputs need to be in the form of measurable information, things like: the shape of the wine bottle,

the vintage year, or the grape varietal.

In our model, we’ll describe the very liquid and decompose it into its physiochemical properties, like sugars, acids, and yes, alcohol.

A conceptual model

Now we can assume these inputs are related to our output, meaning a certain composition of physiochemical properties are associated with a high quality outcome.

while a different configuration of properties associates with a lower quality wine.

Try it for yourself....

Moving these model inputs, we might hope to find a combination that yields the highest possible quality, but the only thing that matters for the model is to establish reliable relationships within the data

so it can — in our case — express wine quality as a function of its properties entirely.

We would have a mathematical model that takes a wine's properties as input

and returns some sort of estimate as to whether this wine is leaning towards being good or not so good.

The model reduces relationships between individual data columns to an equation that looks something like this:

This is a rather simple envelope’s backside, but in essence it’s an equation that relates some inputs to an output. In our case it relates the configuration of the wine's components to its quality.

We have the inputs weighted by some input-specific factor which tells the model how much impact the respective property has on the output. The greater the weights → the more impact they have.

But how does the model get these weights? How does it learn which property drives quality to what degree?

Learning by example

In order to teach a predictive model to identify a good wine from a less good wine, we have to take our model by the hand and teach it. The model doesn't have our senses — it can't see, taste, or feel a wine, and we can’t be there for it in the future, to pre-taste each wine.

We all know this process of supervised learning from our own childhood.

Think back to the days when you learned your animal names.

You were probably just sitting in a proudly self-concocted puddle of peas, mash and apple juice leafing through a picture book of animal drawings, while your parents tried to teach you what a sloth looks like.

Any of these shapes could have been a sloth,

but your parents calmly supervised your learning,

by labelling the non-sloths

and celebrating the actual sloths!

And even though not every sloth looks the same,

after many examples, you learned the basic features that make up a sloth.

Now, although you sometimes weren't sure whether that thing you looked at was a sloth indeed — possibly because the picture book was a bit rubbish — you learned to give it a good guess at least.

Soooo...

what's your guess with this specimen over there ?

It is a sloth! (possibly)

What you probably did here is, you scanned the configuration of lines and shapes that made up the animal and gauged how likely it is for this thing to be a sloth.

That's exactly what a mathematical model does, too:

  1. you give it examples (many at best)
  2. you label them with what you want your model to learn
  3. and let it detect patterns...
  4. that help your concluding classification.

So — moving back to our wine example...

we're interested in classifying wines into

...high-quality

...and low-quality wines.

But whatever we do, a single bottle won’t be enough.

Just like a single animal drawing won't teach a child what a sloth is, a model needs as many wines as we can get hold of.

Let's get some...

Each wine in this dataset is labelled as either a high or a low-quality wine. This is the dataset we train the model with, our training set.

Training sets are all-knowing. They have all the model inputs as well as the model output variable — the quality ranking in our case — so they can train the model.

After the model is trained it shouldn’t need these labels and we can test how accurate it predicts each case with another dataset — a test set — consisting only of wines with inputs and no outputs.

To calculate the model, the algorithm first splits the dataset into high and low quality wines before identifying how the characteristics of each group correlate with the output variable. For example, higher quality wines might have a higher alcohol level than lower quality wines.

Lastly it would formulate these results in a weighted model equation.

The data we have

We've covered the process in broad, conceptual terms. To build an actual model, we need a real, sizeable dataset with labelled data!

We’ll use this dataset of 1,600 wines from Portugal,

with each row representing a wine

that received a quality rating, which is based on at least three evaluations from wine experts, ranging from 0 (🥀) to 10 (🏆)

as well as the wine’s specific physiochemical property values.

All these wines are red wines from the Vinho Verde region in the north west of Portugal. Each wine was analysed, and quality assessed between 2004 and 2007 by the Viticulture Commission of the Vinho Verde Region.

The Commission is concerned with guaranteeing the quality and authenticity of regional wines by certifying the wine's origin and making sure the wine's properties stay within legislated value ranges.

As such, "quality" is indeed defined as a measure of taste but strictly limited to a sample of Vinho Verde wines of a specific period. We should be aware of this context and remember that this data won't generalise to every wine under the sun.

Now that we have the data, we need to get a sense of the base shape and correlations.

The shape of the model

Now that we know there are some associations between the physiochemical properties (inputs) and the wine quality (output), let’s see if we can build a model that fits a curve best representing the relationship between the two.

For simplicity’s sake let's say, we want to build a model with a single predictor: Alcohol.

One of the simpler options is to build a so-called Ordinary Least Squares Regression model that predicts wine quality solely on Alcohol, which might look something like this:

The model in this case very much is the line. Reality — the actual distribution of our wines in the scatter plot — is of course more complex as we can see. Our model is rather a reduced approximation of reality — it captures the association's essence — and it fits into a simple equation:

y = m · x + b

y represents the quality output value

x the alcohol input value.

b represents the intercept (the point the line crosses the y axis when x = 0) and

m the slope of the line or in our case, how much our quality goes up if we add one percent of alcohol.

We can now use this equation to predict Quality solely on Alcohol.

Plugging in an Alcohol value of x = 12%, for example, will return a Quality (a y value) just above 6.

That's nice! We can do this for any value, however, we’ll soon hit a problem that might bend reality just a little too much…

Because the line theoretically extends indefinitely into any direction, it can lead to rather nonsensical results, like, for example, quality values above 10 or even negative quality ratings.

Instead, we’d like a model equation that doesn’t produce a straight line.

To get there, we first make things worse, by simplifying our continuous data to a binary variable: 0 for low, 1 for high quality. This indeed exacerbates our line problems.

To win, we need a different model function. One that doesn’t produce a line but something like this...

This is a typical curve for a Logistic Regression model.

We can now express the relationship between alcohol and quality in probabilities from 0 to 100%. For example, if a wine has an alcohol level of 12 it has an 86% probability of being good.

This helps us express the likelihood of a wine being a certain quality: 20% good is obviously not quite as certain as 100% good.

It’s like with a badly drawn sloth. We might not be totally sure about it being a sloth, but we might consider it likely.

A grown up model

These are the basics.

For our full model we draw a curve not only for one but for eleven predictors, which is pretty impossible to display in a single visual as you can imagine.

One caveat of linear models — like linear and logistic regression — is that they model correlation: how things move in the same or in opposite directions.

They won't be able to identify non-linear relationships, like, for example, an Alcohol tipping point before which quality goes up and beyond which quality decreases.

So, model selection is a big topic for itself and would be very much worth everyone's while in this case. Apart from potentially higher accuracy, other model types provide further tools that can be useful...

For example, a model from the Random Forest family of machine learning algorithms will enlighten us with a ranking of which variables are most important in our predictions.

In this case, Alcohol appears to have the biggest impact on Quality, followed by Volatile Acidity and the amount of sulphates in the wine.

You might disagree with this ranking. That's fine — numbers can only tell us so much. Your experience or intuition can be used to question the model, add or remove variables.

But assuming we're happy with this, you can think of each predictor — each variable in your data — as a model knob you can adjust to achieve the highest quality wine — in the model's opinion, that is.

What next?

Hopefully, this exploration helped make machine learning a little more palatable and showed how technology can be used to rate what’s in your glass.

We used a simple linear model to predict red wine quality using a dataset full physiochemical properties of Vinho Verde wines. As a next step it might be interesting to swap out any or all of these components:

Output. Our model looked at what properties defined high quality wines, but what happens if instead of quality we wanted to predict specific taste features or sales figures. What inputs get us there?

Dataset. We looked at one dataset of red wines from Vinho Verde. What happens when we look at datasets from other wineries with different characteristics?

Model type. We used a linear model, what happens when we use a non-linear model, like Support Vector Machines or Neural Networks? Those models might prove more accurate, but could also be overly complex.

Maybe for another day...
